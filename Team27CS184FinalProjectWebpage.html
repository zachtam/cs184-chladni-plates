Abstract

In this project, we demonstrate the convergent behavior of particles on a vibrating plate, inspired from this YouTube video. We started this project by using the cloth piece and visualizer from Homework 4 to model the vibration energy of the plate. The cloth piece was replaced by a wave model-based energy map without the spring effect. We modeled the particles as moving in accordance with the energy at the point where the particles are. The energy map is not shown in the simulation, and instead the flat plate is shown. Finally, we change the frequency and amplitude of our simulation to generate different sand patterns, which creates several Chladni simulations for our final presentation.

Technical Approach

In the final version of our project, we approximate the Chladni plate experiment using 1000 simulated particles. Under the hood, we also maintain a 51x51 discretized “energy map”. To simulate the patterns, at each time step (empirically set to 1e-4 seconds), we:

  1. Vertically move the centerpoint of our simulated energy map according to the standard sine wave oscillation y = A * sin(2πωt) for our selected amplitude A and frequency ω.
  2. Propagate the vibrational wave through the energy map via the following convolution equation (with the edges fixed to y = 0):

Intuitively, the convolution filter propagates the wave, and the y(t) - y(t-1) term carries forward whatever flow has already begun. This empirically produces good results.

  1. For each particle, we then use bilinear interpolation to compute its vibrational wave “height” h from the discretized energy map. We then move in a uniformly sampled random direction by a distance of |h3|, which empirically gives us good performance. We cap this motion at 0.05 meters to avoid unrealistic particle motion. Furthermore, when particles fall off the edge of the plate, we randomly place them back on.

Intuitively, this approach corresponds to vibrating the plate and giving each particle kinetic energy based on how much that part of the plate is vibrating. The patterns in the Chladni plate experiment correspond to where the plate has low energy (i.e., nodes in the standing vibration) because once particles enter those areas they will not move away.

We found that with a higher exponent on h (e.g., energy = |h4|), the patterns would have wider bands (as h can be further from 0 while still having |h4| very close to 0). In contrast, with a lower exponent on h (e.g., energy = |h1|), the patterns would be less stable (as even a bit of random motion could take a stable particle away from a zero energy zone).

We initially built our project on top of the Homework 4 simulator, but in the end we redesigned all of the original cloth and sphere behavior from scratch to implement the above simulation pipeline. Our final codebase uses only the Homework 4 visualizer and the code structure (so that our objects interface properly with the visualizer).

The Journey

Our first approach was to bounce particles off of a vibrating cloth, implemented using the Homework 4 cloth simulator. However, this approach was slow, complicated, and resulted in particles occasionally clipping through the cloth. At each time step, collisions were computed via ray-triangle intersection between the particle’s motion and the triangles of the cloth mesh. However, since we alternated moving the particles and the cloth, the cloth could cross above the particle without a collision check happening, causing the particle to fall into the void. We considered various solutions to detect this behavior, but we ultimately decided to use an approach similar to “bump mapping” due to computational efficiency and algorithmic simplicity: we used the vibrating cloth to compute surface normals for a flat plate, and had the particles bounce off that plate.

This was somewhat effective, but the cloth simply did not look “right” and there were no long-standing discernible patterns. This remained true regardless of how we tuned gravity, damping, and the spring constants. We think this was because of the spring-mesh model, which is designed to look good (not to propagate waves accurately). Therefore, we switched to computing the surface normals from the “heights” computed by our convolution approach (see “Technical Approach” above). This helped somewhat, but the particles did not settle properly.

Following this, we came up with the particle energy strategy that became our final approach. Still, our performance was very variable. We learned from Harvard’s Big Chladni Plate experiment that we needed to tune our plate to very specific frequencies and amplitudes to see patterns. Thus, we added adjustment knobs to the GUI. We also set the exponent of h in our energy model (see “Technical Approach” above) to optimize pattern crispness.

We also looked into closed-form solutions for where the sand should settle, but we found that they did not give us physically convincing results for how the sand would move into those patterns. In the end, we found that simulating 2D wave propagation gave us a good balance of physical fidelity and computation time.

Lessons Learned

Our main lesson in this project was that the “hard part” in graphical simulation is coming up with the right model – not coding it up. We were able to try a handful of different algorithms (see “The Journey” above), and found that even small changes could make a big difference.

We also learned to trade off physical fidelity and computation speed. While in practice, the particles bounce around, the 2D model runs significantly faster. This led us to the lesson that “if it works, it works”: our model is an engineering trick that works very well for our specific project. In that sense, it is a lot like the spring-mesh cloth model (which worked very well for Homework 4 and very poorly for our project 🙂).

We also learned how the Chladni plate patterns appear in real life!
